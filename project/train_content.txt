Why do we actually need sleep?
Sleep is not just rest, it’s essential for your physical and mental health. While you sleep, your brain processes information and
clears waste products. Your body repairs cells, strengthens your immune system, and regulates important hormones like cortisol
(stress), insulin (blood sugar), and growth hormone.
Long-term poor sleep has been linked to increased risk of chronic conditions such as:
Heart disease / Stroke
Hypertension
DiabetesObesity
Depression
Overall cardiovascular mortality
The American Heart Association (AHA) added sleep as the 8th pillar to its "Life's Essential 8" cardiovascular health checklist.
What is the circadian rhythm?
The circadian rhythm is your body’s natural internal clock that runs on an approximately 24-hour cycle.
It helps regulate many essential biological processes, including:
Sleep and wake timing
Body temperature
Hormone release (such as melatonin and cortisol)
Energy levels and alertness
Metabolism and digestion
The circadian rhythm is controlled by a small part of the brain called the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN). This “master clock”
responds mainly to light and darkness.
Light exposure (especially morning light) signals your body to stay awake and alert
Darkness signals your body to start producing melatonin, making you feel sleepy
When your circadian rhythm is aligned, you tend to:
Fall asleep more easily
Wake up feeling refreshed
Have more stable energy during the day
When it’s disrupted, it can lead to poorer sleep quality and has been linked in research to increased risk of health issues such as
diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and mental health disturbances.
What happens in my body while I sleep?
Sleep consists of 4 sleep stages, each characterized by unique patterns of brain activity, eye movements, and physiological and
hormonal changes:
Awake: Even though you’re technically awake, there are still sleep-inducing changes occurring: Your body begins to relax, and
your heart rate and breathing slow down. Muscle activity decreases, and you may experience sudden muscle contractions,
known as hypnic jerks.
Light sleep: Eye movements cease, and brain activity becomes characterized by sleep spindles (bursts of rapid brain waves)
and K-complexes (large, slow waves). Body temperature and heart rate continue to decrease. You become less responsive to
your surroundings. The neurotransmitters acetylcholine and serotonin are active, promoting the transition from wakefulness
to sleep.
Deep sleep: Brain activity changes to show low, high-amplitude delta brain waves. Increase in blood supply to the muscles,
hormone release (including growth hormone), and cellular repair take place. Growth hormone and prolactin are released,
supporting physical growth, tissue repair, and immune system function.
REM sleep: Rapid eye movements, vivid dreams, and heightened brain activity characterize this stage of sleep. Despite the
brain being highly active, voluntary muscle activity is nearly absent, creating a state of temporary paralysis known as REM
atonia. Heart rate and breathing become irregular, resembling wakefulness. Acetylcholine, cortisol (to a lesser extent than
wakefulness), and norepinephrine are active during REM sleep.
How can I improve my sleep based on science?
1. Keep a consistent sleep schedule
Research on circadian biology shows that the human body runs on a ~24-hour internal clock (the circadian rhythm), controlled by
the suprachiasmatic nucleus in the brain.
When sleep and wake times vary too much, this clock becomes desynchronized, which is linked to poorer sleep quality.
2. Get daylight exposure in the morning
Light is the strongest time cue for the circadian system. Morning light exposure increases cortisol production and suppresses
melatonin. Furthermore it shifts the internal clock earlier, helping align sleep with the natural day-night cycle.
3. Reduce evening light (especially blue light)
Evening exposure to short-wavelength light (blue light) inhibits melatonin secretion via retinal photoreceptors. Melatonin signals
to your body that it’s time to prepare for sleep, it helps make you feel drowsy and ready to rest. Blue light exposure increases
sleep onset latency and reduces subjective sleep quality.
4. Limit caffeine intake in the afternoon and evening
Caffeine is an adenosine receptor antagonist. Adenosine naturally accumulates during wakefulness and increases sleep pressure
(the body’s natural build-up of the need for sleep, increasing the longer you stay awake). By blocking these receptors, caffeine
reduces perceived fatigue without reducing underlying sleep need.
Caffeine consumed even 6 hours before bedtime can significantly reduce total sleep time and sleep quality.
5. Avoid heavy meals late at night
Late eating affects peripheral circadian clocks (especially in the liver and digestive system). It can also increase metabolic activity
and core body temperature, which normally needs to decrease for sleep initiation.
6. Reduce physiological arousal before sleep
Sleep onset requires a shift from sympathetic (“fight or flight”) to parasympathetic (“resting”) nervous system activity. Elevated
cortisol and cognitive arousal delay this transition.
Mindfulness-based interventions, breathing techniques, and relaxation training reduce sleep latency and improve sleep
continuity.
7. Keep a cool, dark sleep environment
Core body temperature naturally drops before sleep as part of circadian regulation. A cooler environment supports this
thermoregulatory process. Darkness promotes endogenous melatonin production.
8. Be physically active during the day
Regular physical activity influences circadian timing, body temperature regulation, and homeostatic sleep pressure. Exercise
(especially increases adenosine accumulation and supports deeper slow-wave sleep.

Why do we need movement / physical activity?
Physical activity is not only about fitness or weight management , but is a fundamental regulator of long-term physical and cognitive health.
During movement, the body activates multiple systems simultaneously:
Musculoskeletal adaptation (muscle and bone strengthening)
Cardiovascular efficiency (heart and blood vessel function)
Metabolic regulation (glucose and lipid control)
Neurochemical balance (mood and stress regulation)
Regular physical activity is associated with a reduced risk of chronic conditions such as:
Cardiovascular disease
Diabetes
Obesity and metabolic syndrome
Osteoporosis and frailty
Depression and anxiety
Neurodegenerative decline
The American Heart Association includes physical activity as one of the core pillars of cardiovascular health due to its systemic effects on mortality risk and disease prevention.
What is the role of movement in long-term health and longevity?
Muscle tissue is a key predictor of healthy ageing. Muscle is not only for strength, but it also functions as a metabolic and endocrine organ that:
Regulates glucose uptake
Supports insulin sensitivity
Produces myokines that influence inflammation and immune function
Loss of muscle mass (sarcopenia) is strongly associated with frailty, reduced mobility, and increased risk of chronic disease later in life.
Research consistently shows that maintaining muscle strength and cardiorespiratory fitness is one of the strongest predictors of longevity.
How does physical activity affect the brain?
Physical activity and especially exercise has direct effects on brain structure and function. Physical activity increases:
Cerebral blood flow (oxygen and nutrient delivery to the brain)
Release of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), which supports neuroplasticity
Dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine activity, which regulate mood, focus, and motivation
Regular movement is associated with improved:
Cognitive performance
Memory function
Stress resilience
Long-term brain health and reduced risk of neurodegenerative disease
What happens in the body during physical activity?
Exercise triggers coordinated physiological responses across multiple systems such as
Muscular system: Muscle fibers experience micro-level stress. This stimulates repair and hypertrophy (muscle growth) during recovery
Cardiovascular system: Heart rate and stroke volume increase. Long-term adaptation improves oxygen delivery efficiency
Metabolic system: Glucose uptake increases independent of insulin. Energy substrates (fat and carbohydrates) are utilized more efficiently
Endocrine and nervous system: Stress hormones (like adrenaline) rise acutely. Post-exercise, parasympathetic activity increases, supporting recovery
How much movement do we actually need?
Current guidelines from global health organisations suggest:
At least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week, or 75 minutes of vigorous activity, plus strength training twice per week
However, research increasingly shows that daily movement quality and consistency may be more important than structured exercise alone.
Even light activity (walking, household activities, general daily movements) contributes to metabolic and cardiovascular health.
Sedentary behaviour, even in physically active individuals, is independently linked to increased cardiometabolic risk.
How does physical activity regulate mental health?
Exercise influences the autonomic nervous system by:
Increasing acute sympathetic activation (during activity), followed by enhanced parasympathetic rebound (recovery phase). Furthermore, it stimulates:
Endorphins (pain modulation and mood elevation)
Serotonin (mood stability)
Dopamine (motivation and reward regulation)
Regular movement is associated with reduced symptoms of anxiety and depression, likely due to both neurochemical and anti-inflammatory effects.
Why is strength training especially important for ageing?
From a longevity perspective, resistance training is critical. After approximately age 30, muscle mass naturally declines without intervention. Strength training helps:
maintain muscle mass and strength, preserve bone density, Improve insulin sensitivity and reduce risk of falls and frailty in later life.

Why do we actually need mindfulness and meditation?
Mindfulness and meditation are mental training practices that help regulate attention, emotion, and the body’s stress response.
From a biological perspective, they influence the autonomic nervous system, shifting activity away from a chronic “fight-or-flight” state (sympathetic activation) toward a more balanced “rest-and-recover”
state (parasympathetic activation).
Regular practice has been associated in research with improvements in:
Stress regulation
Emotional stability
Attention and focus
Sleep quality
Overall psychological wellbeing
Chronic stress is linked to elevated cortisol levels and long-term effects on metabolic, cardiovascular, and immune health. Mindfulness practices help reduce this sustained physiological stress load.
What happens in the body during meditation?
Meditation produces measurable physiological changes across multiple systems:
Nervous system
Increased parasympathetic (vagal) activation
Reduced sympathetic nervous system activity
Lower resting heart rate and blood pressure over time
Endocrine system
Decreased cortisol levels in several studies
Improved stress recovery after acute stress exposure
Brain activity
Changes in alpha and theta brain wave activity
Increased connectivity between attention and emotion-regulation networks
These effects reflect a shift toward a more regulated and less reactive physiological state.
Can mindfulness improve sleep?
Yes — mindfulness can improve sleep primarily by reducing cognitive and physiological arousal before bedtime.
It helps by:
Reducing rumination and “thought looping”
Lowering sympathetic nervous system activity
Supporting faster transition into sleep states
Research shows mindfulness-based interventions can improve:
Sleep onset latency (time to fall asleep)
Sleep quality
Night-time awakenings
Do you need long meditation sessions to benefit?
No. Studies show that even short daily practices (5–10 minutes) can produce measurable benefits when done consistently.
The key factor is repeated activation of attentional control and relaxation responses, not session length alone.
What is the key scientific principle behind mindfulness?
Mindfulness works through three main mechanisms:
Attention regulation (training focus and reducing distraction)
Emotion regulation (reducing reactivity to stress)
Body-state awareness (improving recognition of physiological stress signals)
Together, these mechanisms help reduce chronic stress load and improve mental and physical wellbeing over time.

What is the relationship between nutrition and chronic disease?
Chronic diseases are strongly influenced by long-term dietary patterns rather than single foods. Highly processed diets, often high in refined sugars, saturated
fats, and low in fibre, are associated with:
Increased inflammation
Insulin resistance
Dyslipidaemia (unhealthy cholesterol levels)
Higher cardiovascular risk
In contrast, dietary patterns rich in whole foods are associated with reduced risk of chronic disease. Examples include:
Mediterranean-style diets
Plant-rich dietary patterns
High-fibre, minimally processed diets
These diets are consistently linked to improved cardiovascular and metabolic health outcomes.
How does sugar affect the body?
Sugar (particularly added and refined sugars) is rapidly absorbed and causes quick increases in blood glucose levels.
Frequent high intake can lead to:
Repeated insulin spikes
Reduced insulin sensitivity over time
Increased fat storage
Energy fluctuations
However, naturally occurring sugars in whole foods (such as fruit) behave differently due to fibre content, and are healthy.
What is the American Heart Association’s Life’s Essential 8 approach to healthy nutrition?
Nutrition is one of the key pillars within this model of cardiovascular health.
The framework emphasizes overall lifestyle patterns rather than individual nutrients, focusing on long-term behaviours that reduce cardiovascular risk.
A heart-healthy dietary pattern is characterised by:
High intake of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and legumes
Emphasis on healthy fats (such as unsaturated fats)
Moderate intake of lean proteins
Limited intake of added sugars, sodium, and highly processed foods
This approach is based on strong evidence linking dietary patterns to reduced risk of cardiovascular disease, stroke, and metabolic dysfunction.
Research shows that adherence to heart-healthy dietary patterns is associated with:
Lower blood pressure
Improved cholesterol profiles
Reduced systemic inflammation
Lower risk of cardiovascular events

About us
Our Mission
Together, Dr. Nimrat Grewal and Dr. Simran Grewal founded the
TRAIN Health Awareness program to bridge the gap between
advanced medical research and everyday health practices. Our
mission is to empower individuals with the knowledge and tools
they need to take control of their health and prevent non-
communicable diseases. Through our program, we aim to provide
comprehensive information on risk factors, ongoing research
projects, and the latest scientific findings to support a healthier
lifestyle.
Our Vision
At TRAIN Health Awareness, we envision a world where individuals are well-informed
about their health risks and proactive in managing them. We strive to create a
community of informed and engaged individuals who can make better health
decisions with the support of the latest research and technology.
Our Approach
Our approach is centered on integrating cutting-edge research with practical health
advice. We provide resources and information through our website and the TRAIN Health
app, covering topics such as cardiovascular and musculoskeletal health, risk factor
management, and ongoing research studies. By making this information accessible and
engaging, we hope to foster a culture of health awareness and proactive disease
prevention.
TARGet Research Group
Advancing cardiovascular science through translational research, collaboration, and innovation.
The TARGet Research Group is an internationally active academic research initiative focused
on improving the understanding, prevention, and treatment of cardiovascular disease, with a
particular emphasis on aortic pathology and cardiometabolic health.
Led by clinician-scientists, the group brings together surgeons, physicians, data scientists, and
researchers across multiple disciplines to translate scientific insights into meaningful clinical
and societal impact.
Our work spans from genetic and molecular research to real-world data collection and
lifestyle-based prevention, with the goal of bridging the gap between science and everyday
health.

underserved by conventional prevention programmes and to

their health — within the context of
their own communities.

disproportionately affected by cardiovascular
disease, diabetes, and other lifestyle-related
conditions — often at a younger age and with
worse outcomes.

programmes frequently fail to reach these groups
due to language barriers, limited health literacy,
and lack of trust in formal healthcare systems.
Effective prevention requires a different
approach: one that is inclusive, accessible, and
rooted in trust.
Community spaces — such as mosques and
cultural centres — offer exactly that environment.
What we do
Organise community-based health

places of worship)
Provide accessible education on

Facilitate open dialogue between

Co-create solutions together with
participants to ensure cultural
relevance and impact

participatory and culturally
